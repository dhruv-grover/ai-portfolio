# Hypothesis-Led Discovery Framework

**Expert:** John Barrows  
**URL:** [LinkedIn Post URL](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/johnbarrows_i-dont-do-discovery-at-least-not-the-activity-7429268405381242880-UWI3?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAADqypUsBkT9ygcGv9ZhtWV4kokXRCH2LsRo)

## Core Idea

Effective discovery does not begin with generic qualification questions.

Instead, great salespeople research the prospect beforehand, develop a hypothesis about the customer's likely challenges, and use that hypothesis to start a meaningful conversation.

A hypothesis creates direction while still leaving room to learn.

---



## Why Traditional Discovery Falls Short

John argues that many discovery calls begin with questions like:

- "Tell me about your business."
- "What are your priorities?"
- "What keeps you up at night?"

These questions require the buyer to educate the salesperson.

Instead of demonstrating preparation, they signal that little research was done beforehand.

---



## Research Before the Meeting

Before speaking with a prospect, John recommends studying:

- The customer's industry
- Company developments
- Hiring trends
- Trigger events
- Existing messaging
- Business changes

The objective is to identify likely business challenges before the conversation begins.

---



## Lead With a Hypothesis

Rather than opening with broad questions, begin with an informed observation.

Example:

> "Based on what I'm seeing, companies like yours are struggling with X because of Y. Is that happening here?"

This approach:

- Demonstrates preparation.
- Creates an immediate point of discussion.
- Encourages the buyer to validate, refine, or reject your assumptions.

---



## A Hypothesis Is Not the Answer

John emphasizes an important distinction:

A hypothesis is **not** a conclusion.

It is simply the starting point for discovery.

After presenting your point of view:

- Listen carefully.
- Encourage correction.
- Let the customer shape the conversation.

Preparation should guide discovery—not replace it.

---



## Applying the Same Framework to Leadership

John extends this concept beyond sales conversations.

Managers should also avoid entering coaching sessions with no point of view.

Instead of asking broad questions, come prepared with observations such as:

- "Your close rate is strong, but pipeline coverage seems light."
- "Your rep performs well, but late-stage deal control appears inconsistent."

Then ask:

> "Here's what I'm seeing. Where am I wrong?"

This creates collaborative coaching rather than passive questioning.

---



## Discovery as Collaborative Problem Solving

The goal of discovery is not simply gathering information.

It is helping buyers react to informed thinking.

By bringing a researched perspective into the conversation, sellers create more engaging discussions while remaining open to learning something new.

Preparation and curiosity should work together—not compete.

---



## Key Takeaways

- Research before every discovery conversation.
- Replace generic qualification questions with informed hypotheses.
- Use observations to create meaningful dialogue.
- Treat hypotheses as starting points, not conclusions.
- Listen carefully after presenting your perspective.
- The same hypothesis-first approach improves coaching, management, and performance reviews.
